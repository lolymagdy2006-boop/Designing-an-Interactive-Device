Conceptualization & User-Centric Design

Part 4

This is the transition from "paper and ideas" to "code and assets."

The goal of this phase is to build a "ugly but functional" version of the device to prove that the core interaction actually works.

Phase 2: Rapid Prototyping & Tech Selection

I. Selecting the Tech Stack (1.5 Hours)

Students choose their engine based on their One-Page Concept Sheet.

  • The 3D/VR Path: Unity or Unreal Engine. Best for spatial immersion.
  • The 2D/Interactive Path: Godot or Web-based frameworks (Three.js). Best for fast deployment and accessibility.
  • Asset Pipeline Setup: Students must establish a "Folder Structure."
  • Professional Issue: Inconsistent naming (e.g., final_v2_final.png) is the primary cause of project failure in junior teams.

II. The "Grey-Box" Prototype (2 Hours)

We focus on White-boxing or Grey-boxing. Students build the environment using only primitive shapes (cubes, spheres).

  • Objective: If the experience isn't fun/interesting with just grey cubes, "better graphics" won't save it.
  • Core Loop Implementation: Coding the first interaction.
  • Example: If the user "waves," the cube should change color. If the user "clicks," the sphere should move.
  • The User-Center Test: Can a stranger understand what to do without textures or labels?

III. Aesthetic Asset Integration (1.5 Hours)

Once the "logic" is proven, we bring in the aesthetics.

  • Importing Assets: Bringing in models from Blender or UI from Figma.
  • Lighting & Atmosphere: Setting the mood. We use simple "Post-Processing" (Bloom, Color Grading) to create a high-quality feel without complex 3D modeling.
  • Soundscapes: Adding "Ear Candy." Every interaction needs a sound to feel "physical" to the user.

IV. Paper Prototyping & Friction Points (1 Hour)

A "Theory-to-Practice" workshop.

Testing Logic: Students sketch their UI on paper and have a peer "interact" with it by pointing.

Identifying Friction: Where does the peer get stuck? Is the "Exit Point" clear?

Professional Issue: Technical Debt. If the prototype is too messy, we discuss "Refactoring"—cleaning up code before the 12-hour Sprint in Phase 3.

Phase 2 Deliverable: The "Proof of Concept" (POC)

By the end of these 6 hours, the student should have:

  • A running build of their application.
  • One functional interaction (Input → Processing → Output).
  • A "Grey-box" environment that defines the physical space.

A "Tech Selection Guide" to help students decide which software (Unity vs. Web vs. Godot) is best for their specific project?

Tech Selection Guide: Choosing Your Interactive Engine

Choosing the right tool depends on your Objective (Art, Promo, or Fun) and your Target Persona. Use this guide to decide.

  1. Unity (The All-Rounder)
    • Best For: Virtual Reality (VR), 3D Immersive Devices, Mobile AR.
    • Pros: Massive asset store, industry standard, excellent VR support.
    • Cons: Steeper learning curve (C#), larger file sizes.
    • Pick this if: You want to build a "professional grade" 3D experience or a VR piece.
  2. Unreal Engine (The Visual Powerhouse)
    • Best For: High-end Artistic pieces, Photorealistic Product Promotion.
    • Pros: Best lighting and "out-of-the-box" aesthetic quality, Blueprint (visual coding) is great for non-coders.
    • Cons: High hardware requirements, heavy for simple 2D projects.
    • Pick this if: Aesthetic quality is your #1 priority and you have a powerful PC.
  3. Web-Based (Three.js / Play Canvas)
    • Best For: Promotion, Wide Accessibility, 2D/3D Hybrid.
    • Pros: No installation required (runs in browser), easy to share via a link.
    • Cons: Performance is limited compared to native engines.
    • Pick this if: You want your project to be viewed by anyone, anywhere, instantly.
  4. Godot (The Lightweight Choice)
    • Best For: 2D Games, Indie Art pieces, rapid 2D prototyping.
    • Pros: Extremely fast to open/build, open source, very beginner-friendly.
    • Cons: Smaller community/market share than Unity.
    • Pick this if: You are focusing on a 2D entertainment objective or have limited time.

how to make the device usable (Ergonomics) and visually compelling (Aesthetics), while navigating the real-world constraints of a junior developer (Professional Issues).

Phase 1: Part 2 - Ergonomics, Aesthetics & Professional Issues (1.5 Hours)

I. Ergonomics: Designing for the Human Body (30 Minutes)

In interactive design, "ergonomics" isn't just about chairs; it’s about Cognitive and Physical Load.

  • Physical Ergonomics: If it's a VR project, are we making the user turn their neck too much? If it's a kiosk, is the button at a comfortable height?
  • Cognitive Ergonomics: Don't make the user think. Use Signifiers (visual cues) that show how to interact.
  • Example: A glowing object suggests "Touch me." A path of light suggests "Follow me."
  • The 3-Second Rule: A user should understand the basic interaction of the device within 3 seconds of standing in front of it.

II. Aesthetic Quality: The Visual Language (30 Minutes)

Students often confuse "good graphics" with "high-budget assets." In a 30-hour course, aesthetic quality is about Consistency.

  • Visual Cohesion: Ensuring that colors, fonts, and 3D styles match the objective (e.g., a "Cyberpunk" art piece shouldn't have "Comic Sans" buttons).
  • Feedback Loops: Aesthetics are functional. High-quality feedback (a button that squashes when pressed, a sound that echoes) makes the device feel "expensive" and professional.
  • Atmosphere: Using lighting and post-processing to create "mood" without needing high-polygon models.

III. Professional Issues: The Junior Developer’s Reality (30 Minutes)

This is a critical discussion to prepare them for the SAE (Situation d'Apprentissage et d'Évaluation).

  • Scope Creep: The #1 reason projects fail. Students must learn to "Cut the Tail"—identifying which 50% of their ideas are unnecessary for the core experience.
  • Technical Debt: Writing "messy" code to finish quickly will haunt them in Phase 3. We discuss the importance of naming variables and basic documentation.
  • Intellectual Property (IP): A professional issue regarding the use of "Free Assets." Where do they get 3D models/sounds legally? (e.g., Kenney.nl, Google Poly, or CC0 sounds).
  • The Ethics of Attention: Does the device respect the user’s time and senses, or is it overwhelming/disorienting?

Summary of Phase 1 Deliverable

By the end of this block, the One-Page Concept Sheet should now be updated with:

  1. A Moodboard (3-5 images representing the aesthetic).
  2. A Technical Constraint List (What hardware are we using? Mouse? Meta Quest? Touchscreen?).
  3. A Risk Assessment (What is the hardest part to code?).

Professional Checklist

Here the Professional Checklist: Quality Standards as a new note.

This checklist serves as a roadmap for your students to evaluate their own work against the standards of Ergonomics, Aesthetics, and Professionalism. It ensures that as junior developers, they remain focused on the "User-at-the-Center" philosophy while managing the technical and ethical constraints of the project.

Professional Checklist: Ergonomics, Aesthetics & Feasibility

Ergonomics & Accessibility

  • The 3-Second Rule: Is it immediately obvious how to start?
  • Safe Zones: (For VR/3D) Are all interactive elements within the user's natural field of vision?
  • Feedback: Does every user action have a clear visual or audio response (e.g., a "click" sound or a color change)?
  • Simplicity: Is the user required to remember more than 3 steps at a time? (If yes, simplify).

Aesthetic Quality & Cohesion

  • Moodboard Alignment: Do the colors, fonts, and assets match the initial concept?
  • Visual Hierarchy: Are the most important elements the largest or brightest?
  • Consistency: Are all buttons, icons, and 3D assets in the same style?
  • Polish: Is there basic lighting/shadowing or post-processing to create "atmosphere"?

Professionalism & Ethics

  • Legal Assets: Do you have the rights/licenses for all external assets (models, music, code)?
  • Scope Control: Have you identified the "MVP" (Minimum Viable Product)?
  • Error Prevention: If the user does something "wrong," does the application crash or guide them back?
  • Ethical Design: Does the device avoid "dark patterns" or overwhelming sensory input?
